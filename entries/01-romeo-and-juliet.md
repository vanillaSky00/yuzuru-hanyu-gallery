## Opening impression
There is a kind of honesty that only appears before refinement becomes armor. That is what moves me most in **“Romeo and Juliet.”** The program does not feel polished into marble; it feels alive, a little reckless, still close to the pulse. The tragedy is there, but it is not yet distant or literary. It is immediate, young, almost unbearable in how sincerely it is felt.

What gives this performance its force is not simply that it tells a famous story. It is that the body seems to discover the story while skating it. The shoulders arrive before the mind has finished organizing the feeling. The hands open as if they are trying to hold onto something that is already leaving. Even when the line is beautiful, there is a tremor under the beauty, as if the program knows that elegance alone cannot save anyone.

> It is less a retelling of tragedy than a young skater learning how large emotion can become once it enters the rink.

## How the jumps live inside the story
What I like here is that the jump passages do not feel detached from the drama. They are not pauses in the narrative. They are emotional surges inside it. The takeoffs have urgency; the landings do not merely conclude an element, they feel like a sentence finished too quickly. You can sense the youthful impulse to go forward, to chase the music, to outrun fate even while the score keeps warning that fate is already waiting.

That is why the technical side becomes touching rather than cold. The larger jump moments do not create distance from the character; they expose him. There is ambition in them, but also risk, and that risk suits the program. Romeo should not look safe. He should look as though feeling too much has made him move faster than wisdom.

## The face, the arms, the unfinished storm
This is one of those programs where the upper body carries almost as much narrative weight as the blades. Watch the shoulders after a phrase closes. Watch the way the arms do not simply decorate the music but lean into it, plead with it, sometimes resist it. The emotional language is not subtle in the modern, minimalist sense, yet that is precisely why it works. Youth is rarely minimal.

There is also something deeply memorable in the contrast between sharpness and tenderness. A dramatic accent may arrive, then suddenly the torso softens, the head turns, the line becomes vulnerable. That back-and-forth is the program’s emotional truth. Love here is not serene. It is unstable, luminous, and already threatened.

## Why it stays with me
I return to this performance not because it is the final word on tragedy, but because it captures the moment before a great skater learns how to hide effort, how to ration feeling, how to look invulnerable. This program still lets the feeling spill over. It still believes that sincerity is strength.

And that is why the memory of it lasts. It reminds me that beginnings are often powerful not because they are complete, but because they are exposed. “Romeo and Juliet” is full of that exposure: young intensity, beautiful impatience, and a heart large enough to make the whole rink feel too small.
